The Unbearable Weight of Bluey

How a Queensland cattle dog's existential struggle with suburban capitalism secretly encodes the entire philosophical canon — and what her father Bandit is really running from.

By Our Television Correspondent  ·  14 min read

There is a moment in the Bluey episode — I believe it is called "Keepy Uppy," though I may be misremembering — where the family's balloon is kept aloft through collective effort, each Heeler family member batting it skyward in turn. Most viewers, transfixed by the charming animation, miss what is actually happening: a precise allegory for Sisyphean labour under late-stage capitalism. The balloon, of course, is the mortgage.

This is what Bluey does. It operates on two planes simultaneously: one for the children who believe they are watching a funny cartoon about a Blue Heeler puppy playing games with her family in Brisbane, and one for the adults who understand, if they are paying close enough attention, that they are watching a devastating portrait of post-GFC anxieties filtered through the lens of a six-year-old dog who has no idea her father is quietly falling apart.

Editor's Note on Sources

The author watched approximately four episodes of Bluey over two separate occasions, once on a laptop at a moderate volume and once in the background while cooking pasta. All plot references herein are reconstructed from memory and should be considered spiritually accurate rather than factually precise.

We stand by the analysis regardless.

I. Bandit Is, Without Question, the Main Character

Let us begin with the most obvious and somehow still under-discussed truth about Bluey: the show is not about Bluey. The show is about Bandit Heeler, a father of indeterminate professional background — I believe he is an architect, or possibly a marine biologist, it's one of those — who has sublimated his entire pre-fatherhood identity into an increasingly elaborate series of games with his children as a coping mechanism for the slow erosion of selfhood that parenthood demands.

Bluey merely witnesses this. She is the camera. She is us.

"Every game Bandit plays is a negotiation between the man he was and the dad he has become. The dinosaur is not just a dinosaur. The dinosaur is grief."

Consider the episodes where Bandit plays elaborate pretend games on the floor. He always loses. He always ends up on his back, defeated, swarmed by children. Critics have called this "wholesome." I call it a controlled surrender — a man rehearsing, daily, the act of letting go of ego. Bandit is a Buddhist. He just doesn't know it yet.

His wife Chilli, meanwhile, is operating on a completely different frequency. Chilli — whose job I am almost certain is something involving the airport, customs perhaps, though I acknowledge she may be a yoga instructor — functions as the emotional governor of the household. Where Bandit throws himself bodily into chaos, Chilli maintains the architecture of normalcy. She is structure. She is the load-bearing wall. The show never quite asks what would happen if Chilli stopped holding everything up, because the show is, at its heart, too kind for that question. But the question hangs there.

II. Bingo and the Tragedy of the Second Child

Bluey's younger sister Bingo is a Red Heeler and, I want to be very precise here, the most tragic figure in the entire show. This is not a common reading. Most people find Bingo delightful. She is delightful. She is also a child who will spend her entire life slightly behind her older sister, perpetually half a step from the centre of the story, adorable and beloved and always, always, Bluey's younger sister.

There is an episode — I am calling it "The Dance One" — where Bingo does something independently remarkable and the whole family celebrates. I may be inventing this episode. But if it exists, and I believe it does in some form, then my point stands: the show is aware of Bingo's predicament. The show loves Bingo precisely because it understands what it is doing to her.

A Clarification

Upon reflection, there is a strong possibility that "The Dance One" is actually an episode about Mackenzie, a male Border Collie who appears in several episodes. Mackenzie's role in the show's symbolic economy will be addressed in a forthcoming essay, tentatively titled: "Mackenzie and the Border Collie Problem: Exceptionalism at the Edges of the Heeler World."

III. The Heeler House as Philosophical Space

The family home in Bluey is, architecturally, impossible. Its rooms expand and contract based on narrative need. The backyard is sometimes enormous — large enough for elaborate camping simulations — and sometimes small, hemmed in by suburban fencing. This is not a continuity error. This is the show telling us that space in the Heeler house is emotional, not physical. The backyard is as big as the game requires it to be. The lounge room is as small as intimacy demands.

In this way, the house operates like the unconscious. It is a mind with rooms. The kitchen, I would argue, represents the reality principle — things must be cooked, dishes must be done, meals must happen on schedule. The backyard is pure id. The children's bedroom, with its nighttime conversations and anxieties about death and time (I am almost certain there is a scene where Bluey asks about death, or possibly this was a different show), is the superego.

Bandit, crucially, is most comfortable in the backyard. Draw your own conclusions.

"The Heeler house is not a house. It is a diagram of a marriage at a particular moment in history, rendered in pastel and scored with a surprisingly affecting orchestral soundtrack."

IV. What the Grandparents Mean

Nana and Pa appear in several episodes, and their presence is always faintly elegiac. They are older dogs. Their fur is going grey around the muzzle — or at least I imagine it is, I cannot specifically remember their fur colouring at this time. They represent, with quiet insistence, the fact that this period of the children's lives is temporary. Nana and Pa were once Bandit and Chilli. They played the games. They held the balloon aloft.

And now they come for Sunday dinner and watch their son collapse onto the floor for the hundredth time in a dinosaur costume, and something passes across their faces that the children cannot read but the adults in the audience absolutely can. It is not sadness exactly. It is recognition. It is the specific feeling of watching someone you love live through something that you already know the shape of.

The show puts this in there. For the parents watching. At 7pm. After a long day. The absolute audacity of it.

V. Brisbane as Character

The show is set in Brisbane, Queensland, and this matters enormously. Brisbane is not Sydney. Brisbane is not Melbourne. Brisbane is a subtropical city with an easy relationship with the outdoors, a slightly informal culture, and a sense that things can be figured out on the back verandah with a cold drink. Bluey is suffused with this. The ease of the parenting, the willingness to let children lead, the outdoor games in warm light — all of this is Brisbane. The show could not be set anywhere else.

I have not been to Brisbane. But I am confident in this reading.

Geographical Note

Several colleagues have suggested the show may be set in a fictional suburb rather than Brisbane proper. This does not change the analysis. If anything, a fictional Brisbane is more Brisbane than Brisbane. This is how myth works.

VI. The Ending of Every Episode, and What It Means

Every episode of Bluey ends with resolution. The game concludes. The lesson lands. The family is intact. This is, on one reading, the fundamental grammar of children's television: comfort, closure, safety.

On another reading, it is the show making a radical argument: that ordinary days are sufficient. Not extraordinary ones. Not the holidays or the birthdays or the milestones — though those happen too — but specifically the Tuesday afternoon where nothing occurred except a game that got too complicated and someone cried and then everyone was fine. Bluey insists, episode after episode, that this is enough. That this is, in fact, everything.

For a show ostensibly about a dog who plays pretend, this is a genuinely moving philosophical position. Somewhere between Stoicism and something Winnicott probably wrote about, Bluey has arrived at a theory of the good life: it is small, it is warm, it is interrupted constantly by children, and the balloon, eventually, always falls.

You bat it back up.

That's the whole show.
