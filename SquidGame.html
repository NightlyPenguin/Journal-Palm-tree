The Unbearable Weight of Squid Game

How a show about financially distressed adults murdering each other at children's games is actually a tender meditation on male friendship, the symbolism of the colour pink, and why the real squid game was the bonds we formed along the way.

By Our Senior Correspondent for Extreme Television  ·  16 min read

The first thing you need to understand about Squid Game is that nobody is actually playing Squid Game. In the entirety of the show's run — across both seasons, or however many there are now, I believe the number is either two or four — the actual game of Squid Game appears only briefly, at the end, like a thesis statement placed incorrectly at the conclusion of an essay. The whole show is named after a game that functions more as a vibe than a plot device. This is, I want to be clear, a compliment. Squid Game is named after an idea. The idea is: what if childhood, but deadly.

This is the central irony the show hinges on — and it is an irony that creator Hwang Dong-hyuk, whose name I am relatively confident I have spelled correctly, deploys with absolute precision. The games the contestants play are the games of children: marbles, tug of war, the glass bridge one, Red Light Green Light. They are games that children win through luck and instinct and zero fear of consequence. Adults, burdened by debt and self-awareness and an understanding of mortality, are catastrophically bad at them. This is, perhaps, the show's thesis: capitalism has made us too anxious to even play properly anymore. The debt is the reason you flinch. The flinch is the reason you die.

Disclosure of Viewing Conditions

The author watched Season 1 of Squid Game in full, over two sittings, one of which was at a friend's house where conversation frequently interrupted the viewing experience. Season 2 was watched partially, at approximately 1.3x speed, during a period of personal busy-ness.

All character names in this essay should be understood as approximations. "The main guy" is referred to throughout as Gi-hun, which the author believes is correct but cannot confirm with full confidence.

The marbles episode made the author cry. The author stands by every word that follows regardless.

I. Gi-hun and the Question of Whether He Is a Good Person

Gi-hun — Player 456, our protagonist, a man whose hair turns red at some point in a gesture so symbolically heavy it practically falls through the floor — is not, if we are being precise, a good person at the start of the show. He is a gambler. He is an absent father. He borrows money from his elderly mother to bet on horses, which is a level of specificity in the writing that feels almost cruel. He has the hangdog quality of a man who has been making small wrong choices for so long that they have compounded into a large wrong life.

And yet. And yet. The show loves him. We love him. This is the first magic trick Squid Game performs: it makes you root for someone whose entire situation is, technically, his own fault. The show does this by making the system that produced him so obviously monstrous that individual moral failings become almost beside the point. Yes, Gi-hun gambles. But also, the economy did something to him first. The show holds both of these truths simultaneously without resolving them, which is considerably more sophisticated than most prestige television manages.

"The red hair is not a transformation. It is a confession. Gi-hun is telling the world: I have seen what you are. I am no longer pretending not to."

— This Essay, with great confidence

His friendship with Sang-woo — his childhood friend, his eventual antagonist, a man who went to Seoul National University and then committed massive financial fraud and also ended up in the games, which is the show's way of saying that education is not sufficient armour against a broken system — is the emotional spine of the entire show. They grew up together. They end up as adversaries. There is something almost classical about this, in the Greek sense, or possibly the Shakespearean sense. I want to say it's like Hamlet but I'm not sure who would be Hamlet. Probably Gi-hun. Sang-woo is more of an Edmund figure. The point is: it's literary.

II. The Guards and the Profound Symbolism of the Shapes

The masked guards who run the games wear pink jumpsuits — the same aggressive, institutional pink as the game arenas themselves — and are distinguished by the shapes on their masks: circles, triangles, and squares. Most analyses note that the shapes denote rank, with circles being the lowest and squares being the highest, or possibly the reverse. I have seen both claims made and cannot at this time verify which is correct.

What I can tell you is that the shapes matter thematically. The circle is the self — closed, complete, low-ranked, individual. The triangle is aspiration — directional, pointing upward, middle management. The square is the institution — rigid, four-cornered, unfeeling, in charge. This is not an interpretation I have seen elsewhere. This may be because it is original insight. It may also be because I have slightly invented it just now. Both possibilities feel equally valid.

A Correction, Pre-emptively Issued

It has been brought to the author's attention (by the author, upon reflection) that the hierarchy may go: circle = lowest rank, triangle = middle, square = highest. Or it may be the other way. There is also a possibility the shapes have no intended symbolic meaning beyond identification purposes, which would collapse approximately one third of this section.

We have decided to keep the section in anyway. The argument is too good to waste.

The VIPs — the obscenely wealthy foreign spectators who watch the games for entertainment in the later episodes — are perhaps the show's bluntest element, which is saying something for a show where people are shot for stepping on the wrong glass panel. The VIPs wear gold animal masks and speak English badly, which is either a deliberate artistic choice to make them seem alienating and inhuman, or the result of casting decisions made under time pressure. Possibly both. They represent capital without borders, appetite without accountability, wealth so abstract it can only express itself through the suffering of others. They are not subtle. Squid Game does not do subtle VIPs. The subtlety is elsewhere.

III. The Marbles Episode, Which Is the Best Hour of Television

I want to be careful here because I feel strongly. There is an episode — I believe it is episode six, "Gganbu," a Korean word that means something like "close friend" or "partner," a word that the episode then proceeds to break your heart with — where the contestants must play a game of marbles, in pairs, against each other. The trick is that the pairs are people who have become close during the games. Friends. Allies. In one case, I believe, a couple.

You watch people who care about each other try to deceive each other out of their marbles while the clock runs down and the loser faces elimination. Gi-hun plays against his old friend. An older gentleman plays against a younger man he has taken under his wing in a fatherly way. The episode is constructed so that you understand, roughly fifteen minutes in, exactly how it must end, and then makes you sit with that knowledge for the remaining thirty-five minutes while it happens anyway.

"The marbles are not marbles. They are every small moment of trust you have ever extended to another person and the knowledge that trust, ultimately, has a price."

— This Essay, now openly emotional

The older gentleman — whose name I want to say is Il-nam, and I am perhaps sixty percent confident in this — has a secret that is revealed later in the show, and which retroactively reframes everything about the marbles game in a way that is either a masterful long-game narrative maneuver or a somewhat convenient plot development, depending on your generosity as a viewer. I am generous. I think it's masterful. I think the show knew exactly what it was doing with him from the very first episode, and the reveal is earned, and I will not be arguing otherwise.

IV. What the Squid Game Actually Is and Why It Matters That We Never Think About It

A squid game, as explained in the show, is a Korean children's game involving an asymmetrical squid-shaped diagram drawn on the ground. The attacking team must hop on one foot through a narrow corridor and reach the squid's head. The defending team tries to push them out of bounds. It is a game of territory and aggression and balance, played by children who do not yet have the cognitive architecture to understand what territory and aggression mean at a geopolitical level.

Gi-hun, we learn, was very good at squid game as a child. He was an attacker. He was someone who moved forward, off-balance, under pressure, toward a goal. This is, if you think about it, his entire character arc described in the format of a playground game. He is still the attacker. He is still hopping forward on one foot. The whole show is a squid game. The squid is the system. The head of the squid is, I want to say, justice, or possibly just survival. He is trying to reach it. He keeps almost being pushed out of bounds.

This reading probably only works if you squint slightly. Squint slightly.

V. Season 2 and the Continuation of the Suffering

Season 2 exists. In it, Gi-hun returns to the games, which he does voluntarily, which tells you everything about either his character development or his lack of it, depending on how you read the ending of Season 1. He has red hair still, or again. There are new contestants. There are new games. There is, I believe, a character who is either a soldier or a former soldier, and another character who is a content creator of some kind, which is the show's way of updating its social critique for the current era.

Honest Accounting of Season 2 Knowledge

The author watched Season 2 at 1.3x speed as previously disclosed. There was a game involving voting, which seemed thematically rich. There was a character the author found compelling but cannot name. Something happened with the Front Man that felt significant.

The author's overall impression was: more of the same, which is not a criticism, because the same was extraordinary. Whether this impression is accurate to the actual contents of Season 2 is left as an exercise for the reader.

The show's great risk, continued across multiple seasons, is that it becomes a machine for producing emotional devastation on an industrial scale — that the formula of "caring about character, character dies horribly" becomes rote. Squid Game partially avoids this by making the horror feel systemic rather than sadistic. People don't die because the show enjoys killing them. People die because the system kills them, and the show wants you to feel, episode by episode, the weight of a world that has decided certain lives are expendable. That is a different register of darkness. It is harder to look away from.

VI. The Real Squid Game Was the Friends We Lost Along the Way

Every person who enters the games arrives in debt — financial debt, primarily, but also other kinds. Moral debt. Relational debt. The debt of unlived lives and unchosen paths. The games offer the possibility of erasure: win, and the money clears everything. The show understands, and makes sure you understand, that this is a lie. Money does not clear everything. Gi-hun wins and he is not cleared. He is just a man with a lot of money and an enormous amount of loss, standing in an airport, watching the plane that could take him to his daughter, deciding not to get on it.

That final image of Season 1 — and I am trusting my memory here — is the show's most honest moment. He has the money. He has the prize. The game is over. And he turns around anyway, because some debts are not financial, and because the real question the show was always asking was not "can he survive" but "what will he do with survival."

The answer, it turns out, is: go back. Try again. Hop forward on one foot toward the squid's head, off-balance, under pressure, because that is who he has always been, and the games did not change that, and perhaps nothing can.

It's a great show. I think I understood most of it.

The author maintains that the marbles episode is a perfect hour of television and will not be debating this. They acknowledge that Il-nam's name may be something else entirely. The shape-rank hierarchy remains unresolved. The squid is still the system.
