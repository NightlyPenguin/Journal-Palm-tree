The Unbearable Weight of Pokémon

How a show about a child who never ages, travelling alone through a wilderness full of dangerous creatures he legally imprisons in small spheres, is actually a devastating portrait of arrested development, institutional capture, and the specific trauma of never being allowed to go home.

By Our Correspondent for Franchise Mythology  ·  15 min read

Ash Ketchum is ten years old. He has been ten years old for what is, by any reasonable calculation, longer than some of the children watching him have been alive. This is the first and most important thing to understand about Pokémon: its protagonist exists in a state of permanent temporal suspension, frozen at the precise moment of departure — the morning he left Pallet Town, Poké Ball in hand, Pikachu reluctantly trailing behind him — and has never, in any meaningful sense, moved forward since. He wins. He loses. He gets slightly better at losing. He acquires new hats. He does not age. He does not go home. The show calls this an adventure. I call it something else.

The world of Pokémon — named, I believe, the Pokémon World, or possibly just "the world," I am not sure it has a formal title and I am choosing not to look this up — is one of the stranger fictional spaces in children's media. It is essentially our world, geographically reorganised into a series of named regions each containing eight gyms, a Pokémon League, and at least one villainous organisation with an impractical uniform and a surprisingly articulate manifesto. Children travel between these regions alone, or nearly alone, battling strangers, capturing wild animals, and sleeping, one presumes, in the wild. Nobody questions this. The Pokémon world has developed, over decades of franchise expansion, a complete and functioning society in which the most normal thing a ten-year-old can do is leave forever.

Disclosure of Engagement With the Material

The author watched the original Pokémon anime extensively as a child and has retained, with high confidence, approximately forty percent of the relevant lore. The author also played the games, specifically the ones with Red and Blue on the cartridge, and believes the rival's canonical name is either Gary or Blue, and that these may be the same person.

The author has not kept up with the franchise past roughly Generation Four, which the author understands is either the one with the time-travel Pokémon or the one set in a desert. Possibly both.

All references to specific Pokémon names should be considered approximately correct. The author accepts no liability for confusing Arcanine with Growlithe, as this is an easy mistake that reveals meaningful information about the author's relationship to authority and taxonomy.

I. Ash and the Problem of a Protagonist Who Cannot Win

The structural problem at the heart of the Pokémon anime — and it is a genuine structural problem, one that the show's writers must have felt like a slow leak in the wall for twenty-five years — is that Ash cannot be allowed to win. Not permanently. Not in a way that sticks. He can win the Alola League, and then apparently does, and this is treated as a significant event, but the show then continues anyway because the show is a franchise and franchises do not end, and so winning turns out to mean very little in the economy of the narrative.

This creates a protagonist defined almost entirely by aspiration rather than achievement. Ash wants to be a Pokémon Master. He has wanted this since episode one. The show has never adequately defined what a Pokémon Master actually is — it is, the show implies, a state of being rather than a title, something you become rather than something you earn — which means the goal is, conveniently, permanently deferred. You cannot achieve a state of being. You can only approach it. Ash approaches it for decades. The horizon stays the same distance away. He is, in the most literal sense, on a journey with no destination.

"Ash does not travel to become a Pokémon Master. He travels because travel is the only state of existence the show will permit him. The journey is not the means. The journey is the sentence."

His rotating cast of companions — Misty, Brock, various others whose names I recall with diminishing confidence as the generations progress — serve a structural function that the show rarely acknowledges openly: they age out so that Ash does not have to. They leave. They go home. They return to something. Ash continues. Each new companion is, in a sense, a reminder that departure from the adventure is possible, that there is a world outside the road between gyms. Ash watches them go and then turns around and keeps walking. Whether this is admirable or tragic depends entirely on how much you trust the world he is walking away from.

II. Pikachu and the Ethics of the Relationship

Pikachu does not want to be in a Poké Ball. This is established in episode one and never reversed. He rides on Ash's shoulder. He sleeps, one assumes, in the open air. He is, in the taxonomy of the show, a companion rather than a possession, a friend rather than a captured creature. The show presents this as the ideal form of the trainer-Pokémon relationship: a bond freely chosen, maintained by mutual affection, predicated on something that looks a great deal like love.

What the show declines to examine at any length is what this implies about all the other Pokémon, the ones who are in Poké Balls, the ones who were caught rather than befriended, the ones residing in spheres on Ash's belt in a state of existence the show has never quite clarified. Are they asleep in there? Are they conscious? Is the inside of a Poké Ball a pleasant environment? There is a Pokémon — I want to say it is Gengar, or possibly a different ghost-type — that is implied to be someone's lost pet, or possibly a lost human, and the show handles this revelation with an almost impressive breezy confidence and then moves on. The Poké Ball remains. The questions remain inside it.

On the Question of Team Rocket

Team Rocket — specifically Jessie, James, and Meowth, the trio who pursue Ash across every region with the persistence of a debt collector and the effectiveness of a regional theatre production — deserve their own essay. They do not get one here, because this essay has a word count and a conscience, but the author wishes to note that James, in particular, is a character of unexpected depth who canonically comes from a wealthy family and chose poverty and a life of minor villainy over an arranged marriage, and that this is perhaps the most relatable decision anyone in the show makes.

Meowth taught himself to speak human language because he was in love with a Pokémon who called him a freak. The author is fine. Everything is fine.

III. The Gym System as Bureaucratic Critique

To become a Pokémon Master — or even just to enter the regional Pokémon League, which is the intermediate step between "child with dreams" and "Pokémon Master," whatever that means — Ash must collect eight gym badges from eight gym leaders distributed across the region. Each gym leader specialises in a single type of Pokémon. They sit in their gyms and wait. They have been waiting. The gym for, say, Rock-type Pokémon has a leader who has dedicated their life to Rock-types, who battles any child who wanders in, who loses to enough of them to hand out badges, and who then resumes waiting for the next child.

This is, if you think about it for more than thirty seconds, an extraordinarily bleak existence. The gym leaders are the show's most tragic figures, more so even than Ash, because at least Ash is moving. The gym leaders are fixed points. They are institutions. They are the bureaucracy that the adventure requires in order to have structure, and like all bureaucracies they exist to be passed through, not understood. Ash defeats them and collects their badges and they are never mentioned again. They gave him something they had been guarding for years and then disappeared from the narrative. This is what it means to be a gym leader. You wait, you lose, you hand over the badge, you cease to exist.

"The badge is not proof of victory. The badge is proof of the gym leader's sacrifice. Ash carries eight small coins of someone else's defeat and calls them his achievement."

— This Essay, now reconsidering everything

IV. Mewtwo and the One Time the Show Looked Directly at Itself

There is a film — the first Pokémon film, Mewtwo Strikes Back, released in 1999 or thereabouts — in which a genetically engineered Pokémon of immense power decides that humans and their habit of capturing and battling Pokémon constitutes a moral crime, and acts accordingly. Mewtwo creates cloned versions of existing Pokémon, initiates a battle between originals and clones, and delivers what is essentially a philosophical treatise on captivity, identity, and the ethics of creation to an audience of children eating popcorn.

The film then has Ash turn to stone, briefly, while all the Pokémon cry, and their tears revive him, and the lesson is retained by absolutely no one because the next film features a different legendary Pokémon and a different crisis and the systemic critique that Mewtwo levelled at the entire premise of the franchise is quietly shelved. But it happened. The show looked directly at the thing it was and said: this might be wrong. And then it continued anyway. There is a word for this. The word is capitalism. I am using it loosely.

The Author's Position on Misty

Misty — the red-haired girl who travels with Ash for the first several seasons, who wants to be a Water-type Pokémon trainer, who is irascible and funny and clearly the most competent person in any room she enters — leaves the show because she is needed back at her gym. Her gym. She is a gym leader. She goes back to be a fixed point so that other children can pass through her and collect her badge and continue their journeys.

The author would like the record to show that this is deeply upsetting and the show did not handle it adequately.

V. The Pokédex and the Ideology of Total Knowledge

Professor Oak — Ash's local Pokémon professor, a man of great enthusiasm and questionable judgment, given that his primary contribution to the narrative is handing ten-year-olds dangerous creatures and sending them off alone — gives Ash a Pokédex at the start of the show. The Pokédex is a device that records information about every Pokémon Ash encounters. The goal, implicit and occasionally stated, is to fill it: to see every Pokémon, to complete the record, to know everything that can be known about this world's non-human inhabitants.

This is the show's most honest statement of its own ideology. The Pokédex is not a scientific instrument. It is a collector's catalogue. It transforms the living world into a checklist. Every Pokémon encountered becomes an entry, a data point, a thing to have seen rather than a being to have understood. The Pokédex is satisfied by the encounter, not the relationship. Ash can have met a Pokémon for thirty seconds, in combat, and the Pokédex will record it as known. This is not knowledge. This is inventory. The show presents it as the same thing.

VI. Pallet Town, and the Town He Will Never Go Back To

Pallet Town is where Ash is from. It is small. It has a few houses, Professor Oak's laboratory, and Ash's home, where his mother lives with a Mr. Mime who has, over the course of the series, apparently become a kind of surrogate family member in Ash's absence. His mother — whose name I believe is Delia, though I acknowledge I may have invented this — is cheerful and warm and entirely unbothered by the fact that her son has been away for years, circling the globe in pursuit of a credential that has never been defined.

Ash returns to Pallet Town occasionally. He is always passing through. There is always another region, another league, another set of eight gyms and one villainous organisation waiting for him somewhere over the horizon. Pallet Town is the place the show uses to remind you that Ash has an origin, a mother, a bedroom with Pokémon posters on the walls — and then the show sends him away again, because Pallet Town is not where the story is. The story is always somewhere else. The story is always about to begin.

He has been about to begin for thirty years. The bedroom is still there. His mother is still cheerful. The Mr. Mime is still in the kitchen. And Ash is still on the road, ten years old, hat slightly askew, looking at the next route marker with the same expression he had on the day he left: absolute, unkillable, structurally mandated hope.

It is, somehow, the most human thing about him.

The author stands by the claim that Misty's departure was handled inadequately and invites correspondence from anyone who disagrees. They acknowledge that the rival's name may be definitively Blue in the games and Gary in the anime, and that this is a distinction they were aware of but chose not to reckon with during composition. The Pokédex is still a catalogue. The balloon is the mortgage. The marbles are the mortgage. The Poké Ball is, almost certainly, also the mortgage.
